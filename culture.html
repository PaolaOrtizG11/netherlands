Name of celebration

Image

  

What it celebrates

 The birthday of King Willem-Alexander and national unity A traditional children’s celebration about generosity and giving

Freedom and peace after World War II

 

Enjoyment and celebration before Lent begins

The arrival of spring and blooming tulips

  

History/ Meaning

 Started in 1885 as Princess Day, later Queen’s Day, and became King’s Day in 2013 Comes from Saint Nicholas, a bishop known for helping the poor Commemorates the liberation of the Netherlands in 1945 Originated from old Christian and local traditions Tulips became important in Dutch culture during the 17th century  

Who celebrates

 

 Everyone in the countryPeople of all ages across the Netherlands

Mainly children, families, and schools

 All Dutch citizens; also veterans Mostly people in the southern provinces Dutch people and many tourists  

Main simbols

 

 Orange clothing, Dutch flags, crowns, street markets Sinterklaas, his helpers, gifts, steamboat, horse Freedom torch, national flags, memorials Costumes, masks, colorful parades Tulips, flower fields, gardens, windmills  

What People Do

 Wear orange, sell things at markets, attend concerts and parties Watch parades, sing songs, write poems, exchange gifts Attend festivals, concerts, and remembrance events Dress up, join parades, sing, dance Visit gardens like Keukenhof, take photos, enjoy nature
